What should you look for in a Hyperliquid bot?

Look first for custody control, strategy fit and transparent API-based execution.

Hyperliquid is a high-performance on-chain exchange with order books for perpetuals and spot, and bots sit on top of that exchange rather than replacing it. The point is not to find the loudest promise; it is to find software that matches the way you trade and respects the permissions model. Hyperliquid's own documentation explains what Hyperliquid offers, while the bot layer is where automation, signal logic and portfolio controls differ.

Selection criteria

  • Custody / trade-only API wallet: the bot should use an API or agent wallet that can trade but cannot withdraw.
  • Strategy fit: choose a bot built for your intended style, whether directional, grid, funding-aware or lower-touch automation.
  • Ease of setup: a ready bot should reduce operational friction without asking for unsafe permissions.
  • Transparency: you should understand what the bot can do, what it cannot do and how to stop it.
  • Perps support: if you trade Hyperliquid perps, the bot needs execution logic that respects leverage, liquidation and funding risk.

What is the Hyperliquid bot landscape?

The landscape splits into ready tools for traders and developer routes for teams that want to code their own.

Ready tools are designed to get a strategy running without maintaining your own trading stack. In that market landscape, names traders may encounter include goodcryptoX, Hyperdash and Hummingbot. Treat those names as examples of the broader ecosystem, then compare the category: custody model, supported strategy types, interface clarity, operational controls and whether the tool is built around Hyperliquid execution.

The other route is DIY. Developers can build against the official Hyperliquid Python SDK, which makes sense when you need custom infrastructure, internal risk controls or proprietary strategy logic. DIY gives maximum flexibility, but it also means you own uptime, monitoring, security, testing and every edge case in the trading system.

How do you choose the right one for you?

Choose the bot whose custody model, strategy type and operating burden match how you actually trade.

If you want a lower-maintenance route, start with ready tooling and compare the permissions, strategy controls and clarity of the interface. If your strategy is unusual or part of a larger internal system, the SDK route can be better, but only if you are prepared to maintain it like production software. Avoid choosing by vague return claims; leveraged perps can move quickly, and no bot can make them risk-free.
